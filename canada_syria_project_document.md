# Canada–Syria Sanctions & Economic Access Program
## Full Project Document

### Project summary

This project is a Canada-facing research, content, and decision-support website focused on Syria sanctions easing, residual compliance risk, and real economic access opportunities. It is designed for Canadian investors, banks, NGOs, lawyers, donors, policymakers, and Syria-linked business communities who need authoritative guidance, practical tools, and a clear source trail.[cite:114][cite:120][cite:147][cite:164]

The core problem is that Syria policy has changed faster than institutional behavior. Canada has eased broad economic prohibitions while still maintaining targeted sanctions, which means users need help understanding what is now allowed, what remains sensitive, and what is likely to be bankable, documentable, and defensible in practice.[cite:100][cite:101][cite:108][cite:169]

### Product vision

The product should be a trusted public-interest platform with a professional research voice and a practical workflow orientation. It should combine official-source monitoring, clear explanatory content, sector opportunity mapping, lightweight tools, intake support, and recurring research outputs such as a sanctions monitor and a Syria-in-Figures data page.[cite:114][cite:164][cite:171][cite:225]

The brand architecture should use an umbrella identity, such as **Levant Policy Lab**, and then house the project as **Canada–Syria Sanctions & Economic Access Program**. That structure keeps the site credible and expandable while allowing future Syria-focused or broader Levant-focused work.[cite:171][cite:204]

### Customer stories

#### Investor and business stories

- A Canadian investor wants to know whether a Syria-linked opportunity is realistic before spending time on diligence, so that they can focus on sectors with a plausible legal and operational path.[cite:105][cite:208][cite:219]
- A Canadian SME wants to sell goods or provide services into Syria, so that it can determine whether the deal is lawful, bankable, and supported by the right documentation.[cite:100][cite:147][cite:164]
- A Syria-Canadian business network wants to present opportunities credibly to partners and financiers, so that it can reduce fear, confusion, and rejection caused by uncertainty.[cite:211][cite:212][cite:125]

#### Bank and compliance stories

- A bank compliance officer wants a reliable framework for reviewing a Syria-linked payment, so that they do not over-block lawful activity or miss sanctions-sensitive issues.[cite:147][cite:125][cite:148]
- A client onboarding team wants a source-backed explanation of the current Canadian regime, so that internal decisions are consistent across teams and jurisdictions.[cite:100][cite:101][cite:164]

#### NGO and donor stories

- A Canadian NGO wants to route humanitarian funds into Syria, so that it can avoid delays, refusals, and avoidable compliance concerns.[cite:148][cite:173][cite:207]
- A donor or grantmaker wants to support Syria work with confidence, so that the funding structure is auditable, sanctions-aware, and responsibly documented.[cite:148][cite:173]

#### Legal and policy stories

- A law firm wants authoritative Canadian text plus a practical comparison to other jurisdictions, so that advice reflects how Canada differs from the United States and the European Union.[cite:125][cite:124][cite:108]
- A policymaker wants to understand the real effect of sanctions easing, so that policy and engagement reflect actual market and humanitarian bottlenecks.[cite:99][cite:100][cite:150]

### Product opportunity assessment

The product is timely because Canada’s Syria regime has shifted into a more nuanced environment. Broad prohibitions were eased, but targeted sanctions remain, leaving a large gray zone between legality and operational reality.[cite:100][cite:101][cite:207]

This creates a market for a product that translates official rules into practical decisions. Government pages are authoritative but not workflow-oriented; law firm alerts are useful but episodic and often client-specific; sector and business platforms show activity but do not solve compliance interpretation. A platform that sits in the middle can serve as a trusted bridge.[cite:114][cite:164][cite:125]

### Site map detail

#### Home

The home page is intentionally compact. It includes the program title, a short value proposition, a visible at-a-glance facts card, and two featured tools. The design reflects the principle that home should not list everything, but rather guide the visitor to the correct section fast.

#### Sanctions & Legal Landscape

This section is the legal baseline for the site. It explains the key Canadian changes, including the 2025 general permit, the 2025 amendments, and the February 2026 easing that removed 24 entities and one individual while leaving targeted sanctions in place.[cite:207][cite:169][cite:100][cite:101]

#### Economic Access & Sectors

This section maps Syria rebuilding sectors from a Canadian decision-maker’s perspective. The first launch should focus on energy and infrastructure, banking and payments, telecom and digital, agriculture and food, logistics and transport, and construction and housing.[cite:208][cite:219][cite:225]

#### Tools & Guides

This page should list all tools, even if only two are featured on the home page. The site should include a “Can I do this?” assistant, a sanctions timeline, checklists for banking and payments, NGOs, investors, and export/import cases, plus a consent and data-use note.[cite:148][cite:164][cite:171]

#### Syria in Figures: Canada Edition

This is the public data product. It should show real numbers such as Canada’s $84 million humanitarian assistance announcement in 2025, the broader more-than-$4.7 billion Canada commitment since 2016, and the Dec 2025 sanctions counts of 56 entities and 225 individuals.[cite:173][cite:100][cite:101]

#### Request Support

The intake page should collect segment, issue type, short description, and jurisdictions involved. It should also ask whether the user consents to anonymized insights being used for research or advocacy, without affecting the support they receive.[cite:148][cite:161][cite:160]

#### About & Methodology

This section explains source discipline, update cadence, and AI use. The site should be explicit that AI accelerates drafting and monitoring but does not replace human review for compliance-sensitive content.[cite:147][cite:164][cite:148]

### Business model

A hybrid model is the best fit.

| Layer | Offer | Buyer |
|---|---|---|
| Public | Free explainers, selected pages, and basic tools | Broad audience |
| Professional | Paid monitor, briefings, downloadable data, webinars | Law firms, NGOs, consultancies, researchers |
| Enterprise | Custom memos, sector mapping, workshops, priority triage | Banks, investors, donors, government-linked actors |
| Partner-funded | Public-interest research and dashboards | Foundations and institutional funders |

This model works because the product serves both public-interest and commercial users, and because policy changes create recurring demand for updates. It also avoids overreliance on one revenue stream.[cite:171][cite:204][cite:125]

### AI enablement

AI should be used for monitoring, classification, drafting, and triage. It should not be used as a substitute for legal advice or as the sole source for compliance-sensitive decisions.[cite:147][cite:164][cite:148]

Useful AI functions include source monitoring, structured summaries, case triage, tool routing, and first-pass data extraction. Every material claim should remain linked to an official or clearly attributable source, and human review should remain the final gate.[cite:114][cite:112][cite:164]

### Product risks

The biggest risk is user over-reliance on the site as legal advice. A second risk is source drift, since Syria policy changes quickly and stale pages could be misleading. A third risk is operational conservatism from banks and donors that can make lawful activity feel impossible, so the site must address operational reality, not just law.[cite:148][cite:108][cite:125]

### Launch plan

The launch should be incremental.

1. Publish the home page and the sanctions/legal landscape section.
2. Add sector pages for energy, banking, telecom, and a few others.
3. Launch the tools page with all tools listed, while featuring only two on the home page.
4. Publish the Syria in Figures page with real data cards.
5. Add support intake, about/methodology, and resources pages.
6. Maintain a monthly sanctions monitor and update cadence.

### Design and UX direction

The site should retain the one-page visual style but expand into a multi-page structure with shared navigation on every page. Home should remain concise, while the tools page, sectors page, figures page, and sanctions page carry the full detail. That keeps the user experience clean and prevents the front page from becoming cluttered.

### Branding direction

The visual direction should be influenced by Syrian branding cues, but the overall site should remain clean, policy-oriented, and professional. The brand should feel credible to Canadian institutions and accessible to Syrian-linked stakeholders. The recommended naming architecture is:

- Organization: Levant Policy Lab.
- Program: Canada–Syria Sanctions & Economic Access Program.
- Recurring publication: Canada–Syria Sanctions Monitor.
- Data product: Syria in Figures: Canada Edition.
