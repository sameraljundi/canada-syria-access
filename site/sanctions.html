Sanctions & legal landscape

What Canada changed — and what still matters

Canada repealed broad sectoral prohibitions in February 2026 while retaining targeted sanctions against listed persons and entities. This page is the legal baseline for the rest of the site.

Last reviewed: 19 July 2026 · Figures as announced Feb 2026

Overview Timeline Eased vs restricted Official sources

Overview

The practical challenge is no longer only “is Syria completely sanctioned?” — it is whether a specific activity is lawful under Canadian law, processable by banks, and defensible under residual listings and AML/CTF controls.

Important: Legal permission under Canadian sectoral easing does not guarantee that a bank, insurer, or donor will process the activity. Operational over-compliance remains common.

What moved

  • Broad prohibitions on many imports, exports, investment, and financial services were repealed (with limited exceptions such as certain chemicals).
  • Activities previously covered only by the temporary General Permit (Syria) were permanently decriminalized under the amended regulations; the permit expired 23 Feb 2026.
  • 24 entities (and listed individuals per the official package) were removed to reduce barriers with state-affiliated actors in recovery-critical sectors.

What remains

  • Targeted asset freezes and dealings prohibitions for listed individuals and entities under Schedule 1.
  • Updated listing criteria covering gross human rights violations and conduct undermining Syria’s peace, security, or stability.
  • As announced: 32 entities and 229 individuals remained sanctioned under Schedule 1.
  • Cross-jurisdiction exposure (US, EU, UK) can still block a Canadian-lawful deal.

Eased vs restricted

A practical framing — not a substitute for reading the regulations or screening lists.

TopicDirection after Feb 2026Still watch
Broad trade / investment / many financial services Sectoral prohibitions largely repealed Exceptions (e.g. certain chemicals); bank internal policy
Listed persons & entities Some delisted (incl. major state-affiliated actors in recovery sectors) Dealings with remaining Schedule 1 listings
Humanitarian / early recovery Broader Canadian legal room after permanent easing Donor rules, counterpart risk, documentation
New designations Criteria expanded (human rights / stability) Fresh listings can appear without sectoral bans returning
